Modern borders are not just physical walls or fences.a 2 The most powerful borders are ‘imperceptible’ means of control, such as data borders. The data border can also be referred to as a smart border in other words. The goal of the ‘smart’ border is not to manage the border in a humanistic way but to track and block movement in real time with high-tech surveillance systems.a 3 The side effects of this increase the mortality rate of border crossers and exacerbate social inequality. 1.2. Space/Access

Borders, as well as airports, are testbeds. Function as the first places where innovations in security systems, legislative exceptions, and supranational authority are tested and introduced, representing the early stages of change that will spread to cities, countries, and the world.a 14 While these advanced technologies and strict regulations ensure efficient flow and safety, they also impose limits on travelers. Since the 2010s, Arrival/Departure cards have been gradually replaced by electronic systems or automated procedures. In the past, paper departure/entry cards were processed one-time without digital records and allowed entry and exit.a 15

During the COVID-19 pandemic, getting a certification to have the data ‘healthy’ was the only way to cross the border rather than the fact that I was physically healthy to go through the airport. The non-face-to-face digital authentication system has accelerated.a 16 Automated immigration screening is boosted in each country, data-based real-time tracking systems are being expanded with the aim of contributing to protecting the safety from public health and unverified migration in one’s own country.a 17 The European Union plans to implement the EES system for non-EU travelers from 2025. EES stands for Entry/Exit-system. It is a system that allows the travelers to shorten their time at border checkpoints and automatically monitor them to cross borders by systematically identifying individuals staying in the Schengen area for long periods. For this reason, people in non-EU must provide biometric data when entering and leaving the Schengen area.a 18



The EES collects, records and stores:


1. Data listed in your travel document(s)(e.g. full name, date of birth, etc.)

2. Date and place of each entry and exit

3. Facial images and fingerprints (called ‘biometric data’)

4. Whether you were refused entry.a 19

We can’t define physical presence and identity. Biometrics are more than just security tools. It was based on ethnicity a 20 and binary sex labels.a 21 When creating algorithms for face recognition techniques via 3D face shape analysis are used to mathematical formulas such as Euclidean distance and Geodesic distancea 22 to capture and calculate facial feature points.a 23 It was initially designed to prioritize white skin and features big data images and there was a problem of not accurately recognizing the faces of ethnic minorities.a 24 For example, East Asian faces were excluded even though higher identification thresholds were required than Caucasian faces to achieve the same false acceptance rate (FAR) in all algorithms.a 25 Now, It has evolved into a sophisticated system that also includes data from ethnic minorities who were disregarded from data, setting a turning point in where we came from.a 26 However, this also implies the possibility of abuse, with people being profiled to be defined as different in certain societies.a 27 This technical solution emphasizes efficiency and equalitya 28 but it still reflects the prejudice of the person who has the decision-making to process the data.a 29 In practice, it can be an intention of maintaining a white-centered social systema 30 and a homogeneous ethic society.a 31 We continue to be ‘data exiles’ in certain areas for political and technical reasons, without being given enough transparent information about how our data is screened for security reasons.a 32


Hito Steyerl’s works reflect the political and social situation and explore the problem of constraints. How Not to Be Seen: A Fucking Didactic Educational.MOV File (2013) paradoxically shows that in a world where all our private and public data are collected semi-forced, we are intertwined with the data of our society and the spread of visual surveillance.a 33 This eventually suggests that even if people cross physical borders in special free economic/trade zones, such as airports, they are still othered and trapped inside borders in a different way than goods. People have used sophisticated methods to cross borders in various forms. The way people are hidden in goods has tended to increase more noticeably as data surveillance becomes more advanced.a 34 Will attempts to avoid data tracking also become more invisible and secretive?


Airports also function as special environments where people must deviate from everyday norms or relationships for a while and follow new rules.a 35 In French anthropologist Marc Augé’s book, An Introduction to Early Modern Anthropology (1995), he contrasts ‘place’ with ‘non-place’ and defines airports as typical ‘non-places’. According to him, it exists as spaces that lack identity.a 36 The airport continuously collects and records specific information to turn users into ‘passengers’ roles. In this process, they are treated as ‘movers’, so individual civil rights and protection can be limited by the efficiency of the system. However, not all passengers are treated equally in this process. We are divided into mobility qualifications according to citizenship and socioeconomic status, some pass through simple screening, and others experience more processes.a 37 Due to these differences in data reading procedures, people of the global LGBTQIA+ communities, stateless,a 38 and undocumented immigrants can be simply identified as binary data, resulting in more marginalization.a 39 This is a prime example of the dynamics of power operating within the space of an airport.a 40
