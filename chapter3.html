3. Nomad


3.1. Settlers or Migrants

Each person who decides to move must have different reasons. I grew up with people around me who were born and raised in the same city, but the thought of having to live in one country for the rest of my life felt boring. So, I felt like I had a life where I was living only for travel. Humans are essentially a migrating species and have a stronger migration instinct than any other mammal on Earth. The study of early human migration through DNA testing is said to be like an incredibly intricately intertwined traffic map.a 65 I can use the example of the Yaghan people who traveled the furthest in human history. Also the Haplogroups which are specific mitochondrial DNA gene patterns,a 66 were studied in the 1980s and 1990s by major scientists Allan Charles Wilson, Mark Stoneking, and Rebecca L. Cann as part of haplotype research. Haplogroups refers to a group of people who share similar genetic patterns. This allows us to track how our ancestors moved.a 67 Having residences and nationalities is considered to be a human condition,a 68 but I think the opposite is true in some ways. Humanity has migrated in an almost unprecedented number.a 69 This may be to force individuals to have a fixed identity in the middle of the current rising problems such as the existential crisis, nationalism, etc.a 70 Is it possible to have a Nomad identity that doesn’t belong anywhere and continues to migrate and live? Modern nomads are concepts altered from nomads of the past. Early Nomads who didn’t rely on the land simply ran away from oppression and exploitation to settle down and make a living.a 71 However, Current Nomads are a variant of the idea of nomads in the past. They are mainly people who can travel to different places and perform their duties through mobile technology, which is for a certain class. This also requires a visa.a 72 A long-term relocation hard to be arbitrary anymore.

Born in a relatively decent time, I have had the opportunity to travel with little worry. However, until just 36 years ago, in 1989, when overseas travel was fully liberalized in Korea, my parents could not get a passport for a simple overseas trip due to the country's poor economic situation at the time.a 73 Now, thinking about it, it seems unimaginable. It’s quite sad to realize that individual needs were always constrained by the boundaries of the state.a 74 And so far, this is still the case. I also thought that growing up in cease-fire country, this could be deprived at any time. Ironically, in a modern society where everything is unstable, the state doesn't seem to represent or guarantee 100% of any individual's safety and protection.a 75 I came to believe that innocent civilians are always sacrificed within the framework of the state, especially when considering war and political movements. Conflicts between countries have changed the air routes that people around the world should share and this makes us re-examine the possibility of our movement itself.a 76 Perhaps, as discussed in Chapter 1, the ruling powers may be attempting to prevent us from being able to physically move.a 77

3.2. The Data Trails

Sociologist Zygmunt Bauman says in Liquid Modernity that contrary to the solid state in which society had a predictable structure after the Industrial Revolution until the middle of the 20th century, it now has values the possibility of growth and freedom.a 78 The onset of COVID-19, The existence of a digital world has moved our lives to more virtual realms. As an advantage, more global communities have been created than ever before, creating a society that crosses geographic boundaries where we had the opportunity to explore our sense of belonging and identity. a 79 Strava is a global fitness service that records and visualizes user data using GPS.a 80 It builds physical constraints into a digital community, giving a sense of solidarity between people with similar thoughts and hobbies.a 81 In particular, it is enjoying popularity due to the combination of the tendency of the Millennials and Gen Z to prefer immediate and temporary satisfaction and the gamification.a 82,83 I've had the experience of this platform merging the boundaries between online and offline in that users have to physically walk to generate graphical data. It seems that we are already living in the world of Cyber-Physical Systems even without using a specific platform.a 84 If fitness apps are a secondary choice for individuals, real-time services and information provided by map apps are essential elements of life for us on the go.a 85
ㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤ
Real-time services are, after all, velocity and accuracy, and users contribute greatly to a company's ability to have vast amounts of data.a 86 Even here, the platform seems to interact with users and exchange precision and convenient equally. Maps want to know our routes and ask about our experiences.a 87 The live congestion service on Google Maps and the cookie consent pop-up on the website are clever enough to induce users’ tacit consent. a 88 This can be seen as an example of data collection under the guise of a feedback loop.a 89 Over the past 25 years, major U.S. tech companies called GAMAMa 90 have been supported by the U.S. government’s social surveillance structure to promote the spread of surveillance capitalism.a 91 Additionally, the phenomenon of data centralization through corporate login systems creates a ‘walled garden’,a 92 which fosters a more asymmetric and manipulated world through data monopoly, exploitation, and control, creating a dominant power even in the digital ecology.a 93,94 After all, big tech companies appear to be platforms that work with users, but we can only contribute to a certain extent and don't have the authority to modify our own data.a 95 This is due to the structure in which corporations use information and advantageously grow their pies.a 96

In Simon Weckert’s Google Maps Hacks, he tested how easily this system could be manipulated. Many businesses rely on map-based real-time location technology such as Uber and Airbnb,a 97 due to cost and scalability reasons, startups ultimately make use of Google's huge mapping system infrastructure as a third Part provider including its own systems.a 98 This leads to the risk of causing secondary business damage under the influence of fake content provided by Google API.a 99 The task that leaves questions about the vulnerability of data and whether its permissions are reliable.a 100 Aren't we living in a virtual world created by a large company, not knowing that it's fabricated environments? It seems to be up to individuals to recognize and judge reality in the flood of information, how the online world builds and makes us believe in the environment we actually see.a 101

As a real case in 2017, Strava updated its global heatmap which is one of the data visualization techniques that aggregates and visually displays public activity data uploaded according to the app's records.a 102 it leaked data on the GPS routes of workers working at secret U.S. military bases and missile bases in Taiwan. a 103 It was an example of a security vulnerability regarding whether users unexpectedly shared publicly, even if they agreed to provide personal location. In the end, digitalization exposes the danger that in a fast-changing society, our personal identity and social relationships are all reduced to data and we don't even know where all this data flows.a 104 Perhaps we are residents who are trapped in the framework of social structures and playing a defined role. Why do we share the specifics of our movements and why do platforms want to store our details. They're continuously collecting where we have been, where we are, and what we are doing.
